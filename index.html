GCSE — write better promptsThe 4-part prompt framework

Great prompts share four ingredients. Use the GCSE mnemonic to make sure every prompt has all four. Toggle "GCSE colour-coded" at the top of this page to see every prompt below broken into these four colours.

LetterStands forWhat it doesExample fragment
G GoalWhat you want Copilot to doThe verb. The outcome. The deliverable."Produce a 2-page research brief on global life insurance claims TAT benchmarks…"
C ContextWho you are; what situationRole, audience, purpose, constraints."I am an Operations Manager at Contoso Life Assurance overseeing 80,000 in-force policies…"
S SourceWhere to ground the answerFiles, sheets, URLs, jurisdictions, or "use your training" if none."Use the Claims Operations sheet in this workbook" · "Use authoritative sources: Swiss Re, McKinsey, LIMRA, Deloitte, regulators"
E ExpectationWhat the output should look likeLength, format, tone, language, structure, must-haves."Reply with 6 bullets in the Contoso Life Assurance voice; end with a 'What this means' section"
Tip for facilitators: Run a 5-minute round where everyone rewrites a weak prompt using GCSE. The contrast is the lesson.

Tips & promptsTake these home

Five prompt habits that make Copilot 10× better

  • Use GCSE. Goal · Context · Source · Expectation. Every prompt. Every time.
  • Set the role. "Act as a senior risk analyst..." beats no role 9 times out of 10.
  • Set the audience. "...for the COO Ops Review pre-read" or "...for a corporate HR Director" changes the output.
  • Constrain the length. "In 6 bullets" or "in 200 words" prevents wall-of-text.
  • Iterate. First draft is rarely the final. Ask Copilot to "make it more concise" or "make the tone more authoritative".

Three things Copilot will not do for you

  • Make operational, underwriting or claims decisions — Copilot drafts, you decide.
  • Replace primary-source verification — always cross-check facts before publication.
  • Access systems it has not been connected to — your tenant, your files, your rules.
